…& about five birds flew off & circled around in the strong wind calling harsh “franks”. Brian “geared up” with tripod & camera after a minute or two, a presumed ♀ came up against the air currents, checked wing primaries & secondaries, tail & feet, landed on our outer bough. From here it walked down the trench with the grace & majesty of a queen, then it settled on its nest & all we could see was an uptilted orange bill over the edge of the nest. A nearby nest, where another bird had taken off from, was better for photography so Brian moved round a little bit. Several times this bird - with a couple of primaries missing - tried an up wind approach, but didn’t make it, this gave us a chance to observe wonderful & graceful aerial manouvers, as it swept up in the air currnet, missed its chance, flew on a few wing beats, turned & hurtled around the edge of the wood before coming up again for another try. The wind was very strong & definitely made these big birds use these tactics, it wasn’t our presence which shyed them off, as when we did leave & well away from the wood, I looked back & watched the performance all over again. Brian managed one exposure. G.E. & I left Brian in a set-up position & looked around the wood at the other nests, all very white looking from droppings & not as large as I had imagined the structure would be. Below the nest’s trees we fond several large pellets, mainly fur (water vole?) & a few bones. George found a nice breast feather.

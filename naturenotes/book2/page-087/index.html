…the site, this time from a hedgerow where Godfrey found a tits* nest of eight eggs.

The highlight of the morning came when we decided to look at the tall & lovely Elm, which stood there in the pasture & where we had seen the Kestrel of previous weeks fly into. I went over to it alone & climbed up, when I reached the first boughs, a bird crashed out from a hole - looking very much like an owl, which I thought it was & flew toward more large Elms to the south of the pasture. I eagerly climbed higher & peered into a hole, which was horizontal & about eighteen inches in, it contained to my sheer delight a clutch of five Kestrel hawk eggs, lovely in their varying shades of port-wine to reddish brown, I didn’t handle them, but paused in admiration & saw several pellets, of which I took two & another from a bough, on which it had stuck, nearby. I didn’t stop at all long because we most certainly want these pair to be 100% successful, I hurried down & back to tell the others & they were equally as delighted as I was. Brian went over & had a look very quickly & then returned to us. Unfortunately there wasn’t enough light to take a photograph for our records.

We watched for a while from a vantage point & was satisfied that the birds would return okay, in the meantime Green Woodpeckers were flying about quite abit & Starlings also, in and out of the occupied Woodpecker holes.

Deciding to have a snack, we made for the pipes over the river [A Kingfisher flew across the meadow.] where we could see the tree quite easily, whilst doing this the bird returned to the tree & then presumed…
